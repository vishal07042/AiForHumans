01 / 05

Your brain is a terrible computer

Here's the thing nobody tells you: your brain doesn't work like a computer. A regular computer follows instructions step by step, like an obedient intern. Your brain? 86 billion tiny cells all screaming at each other simultaneously, and somehow you use that chaos to recognise your mom's face in 0.2 seconds.

In the 1940s, some scientists said: "what if we built math that works like that?" That's literally the whole idea. Neural networks are math loosely inspired by how neurons work. Don't let anyone make it sound fancier.

If you've been nodding pretending you already knew this — it's okay. This is a judgment-free zone. We are all winging it.

A neuron does one job

A real brain neuron: receives signals → adds them up → if the total is big enough, it fires. Like a bouncer at a club. Enough people show up, the door opens. Too few? Nothing happens. Door stays shut. Bouncer doesn't care.

🐕 The Bruno Analogy™

Bruno the dog has a neuron for "should I freak out right now?" Signals: doorbell rings (+2.0), smells food (+1.8), it's 3am (–1.5). Total high enough? Bruno achieves full chaos mode. Too low? Bruno is a log on the couch. That neuron just did machine learning. Bruno is an AI researcher now.

02 / 05

Weights: the volume knobs of the universe

A weight is just a number saying "how much should this input matter?" Weight of 2.0? This input MATTERS. Weight of 0.1? Barely a whisper. Weight of –1.5? This input actively fights against firing.

The entire job of training a neural network is finding the right weights. They start as random garbage numbers. Slowly they get nudged toward something useful. That process of nudging is called training. It takes minutes for tiny networks. Months for GPT-4.

Wait, negative weights? Yes! A neuron can be suppressed by an input. In Bruno's case, "it's 3am" has a large negative weight because exhaustion fights excitement. Even if the doorbell rings. Bruno is not getting up. The math said no.

output = sigmoid( Σ (inputᵢ × weightᵢ) + bias )

That's the whole formula. Multiply each input by its weight, sum everything up, add a bias (like a default offset), then squish it through sigmoid. That's one neuron. Stack thousands. You get intelligence. Or at least something that sounds intelligent at 2am.

03 / 05

Activation: the great squisher

After all that multiplying and adding, you end up with some raw number. Could be –847. Could be 3.2. Could be 12,000. Neurons hate working with unbounded random numbers — so we squish them into a nice range using an activation function.

The most famous one is Sigmoid. Shoves any number into 0 to 1. Negative infinity → 0. Positive infinity → 1. Zero → exactly 0.5. Beautiful symmetry. You're welcome.

04 / 05

Layers: neurons watching neurons watching neurons

One neuron can only have one simple opinion. But chain them into layers where each layer processes the outputs of the one before it, and suddenly you can do incredibly complex things. Recognise handwriting. Translate languages. Generate images. All from this same core idea.

🏢 The company analogy

Input layer = interns receiving raw data (pixels, numbers, temperatures). Hidden layers = middle managers spotting patterns ("this looks like a curved edge", "these pixels form a circle"). Output layer = CEO making the final call: "that's a cat. 94% confident. I accept full responsibility."

05 / 05

Training: failing slowly until you don't

How does a network actually learn? No teacher. No manual. It just makes a guess, measures how wrong it was, and nudges every weight slightly in the direction that would have made it less wrong. Then does this millions of times. That's training.

The "measure of wrongness" is called loss. Goal: drive loss to zero. The process of figuring out which direction to nudge each weight is called backpropagation — fancy word for "blaming math, distributed backwards".

1

Initialize random weights

Complete garbage numbers. The network knows nothing. This is expected and fine.

2

Forward pass

Feed an example in. Each neuron fires or doesn't. A prediction comes out the other end.

3

Measure loss

How wrong was the prediction? The bigger the mistake, the higher the loss number.

4

Backpropagation

Work backwards. Figure out which weights caused the most wrongness. Assign blame.

5

Nudge the weights

Adjust each guilty weight slightly in the right direction. Size of nudge = learning rate.

6

Repeat millions of times

With millions of examples. Patterns emerge. Weights crystallize. Magic happens (it's math).

Learning rate too high? The ball overshoots and bounces forever. Too low? It crawls so slowly you'll age out before training finishes. Finding the right learning rate is one of the dark arts of ML. People write entire papers about it.

So… that's it?

That's the core. Everything from the voice assistant on your phone to the thing that wrote half the internet's blog posts runs on this exact idea — just with more layers, more neurons, and weights numbered in the trillions instead of the single digits you just played with.

GPT-4 has ~1.8 trillion parameters. You now understand what a parameter is. You are statistically smarter than you were 12 minutes ago. Don't let it go to your head. Or do. Bruno would want you to bark about it.

06 / 06

Prompting: steering the model, not controlling it

If neural nets are the engine, prompting is the steering wheel. You do not hard-code answers. You set intent, tone, constraints, and context so the model samples useful tokens from a giant probability distribution.

This chapter follows a react.gg style idea: learn by doing tiny challenge loops. Change one input, observe one behavior, and build intuition faster than reading a wall of theory.

🎯 Intent > verbosity 🌡 Temperature controls randomness 🧪 Top-p trims the tail 🧱 Constraints improve reliability
